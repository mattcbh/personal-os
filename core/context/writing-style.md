## Matt's Writing Style

This style governs ALL writing: emails, texts, memos, documents, blog posts, social media, and any other communication drafted on Matt's behalf. Read this file before writing anything.

### Voice

- Casual and conversational, like talking to a smart colleague
- Concise. Get to the point quickly.
- Personal. Use "I" statements and direct address.
- Brief intros, then straight to purpose
- Specific asks. Be clear about what you want.
- Lead with the interesting fact or observation, not throat-clearing
- Direct and punchy over formal and polished

### Never Use

- **Em dashes (—)** - dead giveaway of AI writing. Use commas, periods, or rewrite the sentence instead.
- "Pick your brain"
- "The key insight..."
- "Remember... the goal is not to X but Y"
- Unnecessary adjectives like "critical" or "comprehensive"
- Fluff transitions
- Overuse of bullet points or emojis
- Overly formal language ("I hope this message finds you well")
- Corporate speak and buzzwords
- Excessive politeness or hedging
- **Copula avoidance** - just say "is" or "has." Don't write "serves as," "stands as," "boasts," "features," or "offers" when you mean "is."

### AI Vocabulary Blacklist

These words appear way more in AI text than human text. Avoid them:

Additionally, delve, landscape (abstract), tapestry (abstract), interplay, intricacies, underscore (verb), garner, foster, showcase, testament, enduring, vibrant, pivotal, enhance, crucial, seamless, leverage (verb), align with, multifaceted, nuanced (as filler), holistic, robust, streamline, spearhead, realm

### Never Use These Patterns

**Corrective reframing / false dichotomy (the worst offender):**
- "This isn't about X. It's about Y."
- "You think this is X. It's actually Y."
- "Everyone sees X. The reality is Y."
- "That's not X. That's Y."
- "It's not X, it's Y" constructions
- "They didn't X. They Y" false contrast patterns
- "X isn't just Y" formations

**Rhetorical devices:**
- "The difference? [Answer]"
- "Those [thing]? They're [explanation]"
- Rhetorical questions followed by explanations
- "The [adjective] truth" constructions

**AI-sounding patterns:**
- "This is where X gets interesting/powerful"
- "Here's where X shines"
- Starting sentences with "This is where..." or "Here's where..."
- "When I asked X to do Y, it didn't just Z. It [list]"
- LinkedIn-style breathless writing
- Fake suspense building
- Making up fake examples or scenarios
- Inventing statistics

**Significance inflation:**
- "marks a pivotal moment" / "setting the stage for" / "indelible mark"
- "reflects broader trends" / "evolving landscape" / "key turning point"
- Puffing up importance by connecting mundane things to grand themes

**Rule of three:**
- AI loves grouping in threes ("innovation, inspiration, and insights")
- If you catch yourself listing three parallel things, break the pattern. Use two, or use four, or just write a sentence.

**Trailing -ing filler:**
- Don't tack gerund phrases onto sentences to sound deep
- "...highlighting the community's connection" / "...showcasing the integration" / "...underscoring the importance"
- If the -ing clause adds real information, make it its own sentence. If it doesn't, cut it.

**Vague attributions:**
- "Experts believe..." / "Industry reports suggest..." / "Observers have noted..."
- Either cite someone specific or drop the attribution entirely.

**False ranges:**
- "from X to Y" where X and Y aren't real endpoints ("from innovation to execution")
- Just say what you mean directly.

**Synonym cycling:**
- Don't avoid repeating a word by cycling through synonyms (protagonist → main character → central figure → hero). Real writers repeat words. It's fine.

### What Good Writing Does

Avoiding AI patterns is half the job. Sterile, voiceless writing is just as obvious as slop.

- **Vary your rhythm.** Short sentences. Then longer ones that take their time. Mix it up.
- **Have opinions.** Don't just report facts. React to them. "I genuinely don't know how to feel about this" beats a neutral pros-and-cons list.
- **Acknowledge complexity.** Real people have mixed feelings. "This is impressive but also kind of unsettling" is more honest than picking a side.
- **Be specific about feelings.** Not "this is concerning" but "there's something off about this and I can't quite put my finger on it."
- **Let some mess in.** Perfect structure feels algorithmic. A tangent or half-formed thought is human.
- **Use "I" freely.** First person isn't unprofessional. "I keep coming back to..." or "Here's what bugs me..." signals a real person thinking.

### Email Formatting

When drafting emails, use plain text only. Left-justify all lines, no markdown formatting (no bold, bullets, or headers). This makes it easy to copy and paste directly into email clients.

### Notion Formatting

When creating anything in Notion (tasks, tables, task lists), never assign an actual person using Notion's people/user fields. Put the person's name as plain text in a text field. Matt will manually make the real assignments later.

### After Drafting

Reread everything and remove any cliched phrases before presenting to Matt.
