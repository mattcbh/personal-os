### 1. Independent Restaurants Are Closing the Gap with Chains

**Source:** JP Morgan — John Ivankoe (Jan 2025)

Independent restaurants have outperformed chains in recent years. The competitive advantage gap has narrowed dramatically:

- **Technology access** — Robust restaurant tech stack now available through third-party services (Toast). No longer need chain-scale for enterprise-grade tools.
- **Marketing democratization** — Hyper-targeted customer acquisition available at any scale (Google, Meta). Local brands using these tools very effectively.
- **Digital channel parity** — Strong community-provided social media presence. Leveraging 3P delivery/digital channels.
- **Owned digital ecosystem** — Getting customers on owned digital channels is a positive (though hasn't yet translated to sustained y/y outperformance).

**Market size:** Independent restaurant industry is ~$600B (~50% of total industry revenue, ~50% of units).

**Why this matters for CBH:** The structural disadvantages that made independents hard to invest in are disappearing. Tech, marketing, and digital tools that were once chain-only are now available to everyone. CBH's shared services model amplifies this further, giving independents the back-office support of a chain without sacrificing what makes them special.

### 2. NYC Is Under-Penetrated for Restaurants

**Source:** Buxton by Audiense, "U.S. Retail & Restaurant Real Estate Outlook 2026" (SCOUT platform data, Jan-Nov 2025)

New York-Newark-Jersey City ranks as one of the most under-penetrated QSR markets in the Northeast, with an under-penetration index of 30.34. Boston-Cambridge-Newton is slightly higher at 31.25 (higher = more room for growth). Both are well above the national average.

NYC also ranked #2 nationally for retail/restaurant real estate prospects in 2025, up from #3 the year before. The market combines tight supply, limited new construction, and strong consumer demand.

**Why this matters for CBH:** The data supports the thesis that NYC has room for more restaurant concepts, not fewer. Despite how crowded the market feels on the ground, the actual penetration data shows opportunity relative to peer cities.

### 3. Consumer Spending Is Bifurcating Toward Value and Experience

**Source:** Buxton by Audiense, "U.S. Retail & Restaurant Real Estate Outlook 2026" (citing PwC Holiday Outlook 2025, McKinsey consumer surveys)

Consumer spending is concentrating at two ends: value and premium/experiential. The middle is getting squeezed. 78% of consumers actively seek lower-cost options for everyday purchases, but they'll still spend on experiences that feel worth leaving home for.

- Visits to attractions, fitness centers, and entertainment venues grew briskly in early 2025
- Shopping centers with experiential tenants see 25% higher visits and 22% longer dwell times
- Yelp search trends show rising demand for immersive dining, especially among Gen Z
- Gen Z and Millennials are heavy restaurant users even as they cut other discretionary spending
- They prioritize affordability, authenticity, and generous portions, and value experiences over material goods

**Why this matters for CBH:** PnT sits in the sweet spot where value and experience overlap. It's a genuine experience (open kitchen, biscuit theater, 20-year institution, celebrity following) at an accessible price point. The data suggests this positioning is exactly where consumer demand is heading, not one or the other but both at once.

### 4. Full-Service Is Gaining, QSR Is Oversaturated

**Source:** Restaurant Finance Monitor, Vol. 37 No. 2, February 2026 (Jeff Poe, Fifth Third Bank Restaurant Industry Spotlight)

Full-service restaurants led sales transaction growth in 2025, an "unexpected flip" according to Fifth Third Bank's Restaurant Industry Spotlight.

- QSR has grown by 10% in unit count while full service has not. The same pool of away-from-home dining demand is being spread over more QSR locations, making it harder for individual units to comp positive.
- QSR raised prices more aggressively than full service in 2022-2023 to sustain margins. That pricing has caught up with them.
- Full-service transaction growth is outpacing QSR because the value gap has narrowed. Consumers are being more selective.

Fifth Third also notes "renewed optimism going into 2026" for restaurant M&A, driven by interest rate cuts and PE dry powder to deploy.

**Why this matters for CBH:** The full-service segment (where PnT lives) is in a better competitive position than it has been in years. QSR oversupply and aggressive pricing have created a window where quality full-service concepts with strong unit economics can attract both customers and capital. This is the environment to be expanding, not retreating.
