# The Corner Booth Story: A Storytelling Guide

## How to Use This Guide

Before any meeting, lunch, gathering, or party where you might tell your story, scan the relevant audience section. Each section gives you: (1) what this audience actually cares about, (2) your opening hook, (3) the key beats to hit, (4) concrete stories and scenes to draw from, and (5) what to avoid.

**Remember: you're not reciting a script.** You're loading the right images, stories, and emotional beats into your head before you walk in. The words will come naturally.

---

## Part 1: Your Core Story (The Foundation)

### The Origin Scene

**The image:** A kid walking into yet another new school, scanning the cafeteria for an open seat, wondering if this place will feel like home. Five schools in five years.

**The feeling:** Never quite belonging. Always the new kid. Always searching for a place that felt like yours.

**The connection:** That feeling never fully left. It became the engine for everything—Gimlet, Corner Booth, all of it. You're building the places you wished existed when you were that kid.

### The Curiosity Gap

The Heath brothers say the best stories create a question the listener needs answered. Yours is built-in:

> *"I sold a podcast company to Spotify. Now I own a fried chicken restaurant and an ice cream shop across from an elementary school. Why?"*

That's unexpected. It violates the pattern people expect (tech entrepreneur → another tech thing). Use it. Let the gap do the work.

### The Three Concrete Scenes

Abstractions are forgettable. Scenes stick. Here are the three you can always draw from:

**Scene 1: Pies 'n' Thighs on a Sunday**

The line out the door. The same families who've been coming for 20 years. There's a couple who had their first date there—now they bring their kids every Sunday. The smell of fried chicken. The sound of the kitchen. The server who knows everyone's name. This isn't a restaurant. It's a living room that happens to serve food. And in March 2026, Pies 'n' Thighs celebrates its 20th anniversary—two decades of being Williamsburg's living room.

**Scene 2: Heaps on a Summer Afternoon**

A hot summer day. The bell rings at PS 321 across the street, and within minutes, kids are pressed against the glass, pointing at donut flavors. Parents grab coffee while their kids argue over sprinkles. There's the kid celebrating their birthday—everyone gets a free scoop on their birthday, and the place erupts in singing. There's the man who came in the day he became an American citizen, still wearing his sport coat, American flag in hand, ordering his first ice cream as a citizen. This is what community looks like—a place people go to mark the moments that matter.

**Scene 3: The Quiet Moment After Close**

A restaurant owner, alone, doing the books at 11pm. The weight of payroll, rent, food costs, equipment repairs. Twenty years of love for a place—but the economics are brutal. One bad month away from losing everything. These are the people you're trying to help. Not to "acquire their assets." To lift the weight so they can keep doing what they love.

### The Core Statement

If you had to boil it down to one sentence, it's this:

> ***"I'm building the home I never had as a kid—one neighborhood restaurant at a time."***

## Part 1.5: Your Philosophical Foundation

### The Worldview Clash (Wolfgang's Third Layer)

Before you walk into any room, ground yourself in the philosophical tension. Wolfgang Hammer says every great story requires three layers: External (what you're doing), Emotional (why you), and Philosophical (the worldview you're challenging). These are the beliefs you're standing against:

#### Point 1: On Restaurant Economics

**Everyone believes:** Restaurants are bad businesses—low margins, high failure rates, fundamentally uninvestable.

**I believe:** With the right brands and disciplined operation, restaurants generate exceptional returns. The perception of "bad business" creates a pricing arbitrage. Great restaurants trade at 3-4x EBITDA when they should be 6-8x.

**Why this matters:** This is about seeing value others miss. The market is wrong about restaurants the same way the market was once wrong about podcasts.

#### Point 2: On Time Horizons

**Everyone believes:** You need to scale fast, flip in 5-7 years, generate 3-5x returns for your fund, move on to the next deal.

**I believe:** The most valuable restaurant brands—Raising Cane's, Chick-fil-A, Five Guys—were built with decades of patience. Patient capital gets the best real estate, the best people, and builds brands that compound. This creates higher AUVs, greater profitability, and more durable value.

**Why this matters:** The PE playbook optimizes for fund cycles, not for building things that last. I'm optimizing for the latter.

#### Point 3: On Scale and Soul

**Everyone believes:** Growth requires standardization. You need to strip out uniqueness to scale efficiently. That's just how operations work.

**I believe:** The soul of a brand IS its competitive advantage. Authenticity at scale is hard but possible. The brands that matter most are the ones that refuse to compromise what made them special in the first place.

**Why this matters:** Most "scaling" destroys value by destroying differentiation. We're proving you can grow without gutting what makes something worth growing.

#### Point 4: On Physical vs. Digital

**Everyone believes:** Commerce is moving digital. Physical retail is declining. Relationships happen through screens. That's the future.

**I believe:** Physical community spaces become MORE valuable as life digitizes. As everything fragments into feeds and algorithms, the hunger for tangible, face-to-face connection intensifies. This isn't nostalgia—it's counterbalance.

**Why this matters:** Everyone is zigging toward digital. We're zagging toward physical. Contrarian positions are where value is created.

#### Point 5: On Community as Asset

**Everyone believes:** "Community" is marketing speak. What matters is transaction volume, average check size, throughput.

**I believe:** Real community is defensible moat. When a restaurant becomes infrastructure—when the same families show up for 20 years—that's a business that survives recessions, competition, and everything else. You can't disrupt belonging.

**Why this matters:** We're not buying restaurants. We're buying places where people feel like they belong. That's a different kind of asset.

---

**Use this before any important conversation.** Load these tensions into your head. When someone pushes back on restaurants being a good investment, you're not defensive—you're articulating a clear philosophical position they haven't considered.

## Part 2: Audience-Specific Guides

---

### Audience 1: Potential Investors
*(Angels, family offices, high-net-worth individuals)*

#### What They Actually Care About
- Is this person credible? Can they execute?
- Is this a real business or a hobby?
- What's the thesis? Why does this work?
- Is the founder in it for the long haul?
- Does this align with my values/interests?

#### Your Opening Hook

> *"Most restaurant acquisitions are done by private equity firms that gut what made the place special. I'm doing the opposite—building a company that helps beloved neighborhood restaurants stay themselves."*

#### Key Beats to Hit

- **Credibility first:** "I co-founded Gimlet Media, sold it to Spotify. I know what it takes to build something."
- **The thesis:** "Single-unit restaurants are almost impossible economically. But the same restaurants with shared back-office support, group purchasing, and capital access? Suddenly viable."
- **The differentiation:** "PE buys restaurants to flip them or franchise them. I'm buying restaurants to keep them exactly what they are—just with better economics."
- **The personal stake:** "I went to five schools in five years as a kid. Never belonged anywhere. This isn't just a business—it's about building places that make people feel like they belong."
- **The proof points:** "Pies 'n' Thighs has been in Williamsburg for 20 years—it celebrates its 20th anniversary in March 2026. We're opening a second location in Prospect Heights, under construction now, opening March 2026. Heaps is across from PS 321—it's a neighborhood institution."

#### Concrete Scene to Use

Use **Scene 3 (The Quiet Moment After Close)** to illustrate the problem you're solving. Then pivot to how Corner Booth changes that equation.

#### Market Data

For sourced statistics and market data to back up these talking points, see `core/context/cbh-investor-thesis.md`.

#### What to Avoid
- Leading with emotion before establishing credibility
- Making it sound like a passion project without business rigor
- Talking about "patient capital" without explaining what that means in practice
- Over-explaining the corporate structure

### Audience 2: Restaurant Owners (Acquisition Targets)
*(Founders/owners of restaurants you might want to acquire)*

#### What They Actually Care About
- Will you destroy what I built?
- Will my people be taken care of?
- Can I trust you?
- Do you actually understand this business?
- What's my exit? What's my role after?

#### Your Opening Hook

> *"I'm not here to buy your restaurant. I'm here to make sure it's still here in 20 years."*

#### Key Beats to Hit

- **Lead with respect:** "I know what you've built. I know what it took. I'm not here to change it."
- **The anti-PE pitch:** "Private equity buys restaurants to flip them or franchise them. That's not me. I want to keep your place exactly what it is—just with better support."
- **The personal story:** "I moved around a lot as a kid. Never felt like I belonged anywhere. Places like yours—they're what make a neighborhood feel like home. I want to protect that."
- **The practical help:** "I can take the back-office stuff off your plate. Accounting, payroll, vendor negotiations, capital for improvements. You focus on what you love—the food, the people, the hospitality."
- **The proof:** "Pies 'n' Thighs has been around for 20 years—hitting its 20th anniversary in March 2026. We're opening a second location because that's what the brand deserves—not because we're trying to turn it into a chain."

#### Concrete Scene to Use

Use **Scene 1 (Pies 'n' Thighs on a Sunday)** to show you understand what makes a restaurant special. Don't talk about "brands" or "assets." Talk about families, regulars, the server who knows everyone's name.

#### What to Avoid
- Business jargon ("synergies," "scale," "optimize")
- Talking about your Spotify exit (irrelevant and potentially off-putting)
- Rushing to numbers before establishing trust
- Anything that sounds like you're buying an "asset" vs. joining a family

### Audience 3: Casual Social Situations
*(Parties, kids' school events, neighborhood gatherings, "what do you do?")*

#### What They Actually Care About
- Something interesting to talk about
- A quick mental picture
- Maybe a connection point ("Oh, I love that place!")

#### Your Opening Hook

**Short version:** *"I buy neighborhood restaurants. The kind of places you'd be sad to see close."*

**If they're curious:** *"You know Pies 'n' Thighs? The fried chicken place in Williamsburg? That's one of mine. And Heaps—the ice cream shop across from PS 321."*

#### Key Beats to Hit

- **Lead with concrete:** Always name the actual restaurants. People can picture them.
- **The curiosity gap (if they bite):** "I used to be in podcasting—sold a company to Spotify. Now I do this. It's a weird pivot, I know."
- **The emotional hook (if they want more):** "I moved around a lot as a kid—five schools in five years. Never felt like I belonged anywhere. These places are what make a neighborhood feel like home. I want to help them stick around."
- **Neighborhood connection:** "Heaps is right across from the school—so yes, I'm basically in the business of giving kids donuts after school. It's a good gig."

#### Concrete Scene to Use

Use **Scene 2 (Heaps at 3pm)** if talking to parents—it's instantly relatable. For foodies or Brooklyn people, use Scene 1 (Pies 'n' Thighs on a Sunday).

#### What to Avoid
- Over-explaining the business model
- Corporate language ("holding company," "portfolio")
- Sounding like you're pitching
- The full personal backstory unless they ask

### Audience 4: Potential Employees or Operators
*(GMs, kitchen managers, operations people, corporate hires)*

#### What They Actually Care About
- Is this a real opportunity or a sinking ship?
- Will I be treated well?
- What's the culture like?
- Is there growth potential?
- Does this person know what they're doing?

#### Your Opening Hook

> *"We're building something different. Most restaurant companies are about expansion and exits. We're about keeping special places special—and giving the people who work there a real path."*

#### Key Beats to Hit

- **The vision:** "I'm building a company that will own 5-10 beloved neighborhood restaurants. Not chains. Not franchises. Real places with real communities."
- **The opportunity:** "That means real career paths. A GM at one restaurant could become a regional leader. Someone in operations could grow with us as we grow."
- **The values:** "Hospitality, community, quality, joy, connection. Those aren't just words on a wall. They're how we make decisions."
- **The credibility:** "I built and sold a media company. I know what it takes to build something real. This isn't a hobby—it's my life's work."
- **The personal:** "I moved around a lot as a kid—never felt like I belonged. These restaurants are what make a neighborhood feel like home. I take that seriously."

#### Concrete Scene to Use

Use **Scene 1 (Pies 'n' Thighs on a Sunday)** to paint the picture of what kind of place they'd be working in. Then connect it to growth: "We're opening a second location in Prospect Heights. That's the kind of opportunity that comes from being part of this."

#### What to Avoid
- Making it sound too small or scrappy
- Vague promises about growth without specifics
- Over-emphasizing the passion at the expense of business viability
- Talking down other restaurant companies (let your vision speak for itself)

### Audience 5: Industry Folks
*(Conferences like RFDC, other restaurant operators, vendors, industry professionals)*

#### What They Actually Care About
- Are you a real operator or a tourist?
- Do you understand the business?
- What's your angle? What are you actually doing?
- Could we work together somehow?
- What can I learn from you?

#### Your Opening Hook

> *"I run a small holding company—two concepts right now, Pies 'n' Thighs and Heaps. We're focused on acquiring neighborhood institutions and keeping them what they are."*

#### Key Beats to Hit

- **Establish insider status quickly:** Lead with the restaurant names. Show you're in the trenches.
- **The thesis (industry-speak is okay here):** "Single-unit economics are brutal. But with shared back-office, group purchasing, and patient capital, these places can actually work."
- **The differentiation:** "We're not PE. We're not trying to flip or franchise. We want these places to stay themselves for another 20 years."
- **The background (briefly):** "I came from media—built and sold a podcast company. So I'm still learning the restaurant business, but I know how to build companies."
- **Current projects:** "We're opening a second Pies 'n' Thighs in Prospect Heights right now—under construction, opening March 2026. And Pies 'n' Thighs is hitting its 20th anniversary that same month."

#### Concrete Scene to Use

With industry folks, you can be more technical. Talk about the **Prospect Heights build-out**—the construction process, working with the GC, the permitting challenges. This establishes that you're actually in the weeds, not just writing checks.

#### Market Data

For sourced statistics on independent restaurant trends, NYC under-penetration, and full-service vs. QSR dynamics, see `core/context/cbh-investor-thesis.md`.

#### What to Avoid
- Leading with Gimlet/Spotify (you'll sound like a tourist)
- Too much philosophical/emotional content early on
- Talking about restaurants you don't own
- Pretending you have more industry experience than you do (humility plays well)

### Audience 6: Landlords and Real Estate Brokers
*(Property owners, commercial brokers, building managers)*

#### What They Actually Care About
- Can you pay rent reliably?
- Are you a flight risk?
- Will you be a good tenant / neighbor?
- Do you have the capital and experience to execute?
- Is this a real company or a one-off?

#### Your Opening Hook

> *"We operate established neighborhood restaurants—Pies 'n' Thighs has been in Williamsburg for 20 years. We're looking for the right spaces for concepts that will be around for another 20."*

#### Key Beats to Hit

- **Track record:** "Pies 'n' Thighs has been operating for 20 years. Heaps is an established neighborhood fixture. We don't do pop-ups or experiments."
- **Financial stability:** "We're backed by patient capital. This isn't a venture bet—it's a long-term hold. We want to be in your building for decades, not years."
- **Personal credibility:** "I built and sold a company to Spotify. I know how to run businesses and honor commitments."
- **Neighborhood value:** "Our restaurants become part of the neighborhood. That's good for your building, good for your other tenants, good for the block."
- **Current activity:** "We're opening a second Pies 'n' Thighs at 244 Flatbush in Prospect Heights right now. Under construction, opening March 2026."

#### Concrete Scene to Use

Landlords think in terms of **stability and community value**. Use Scene 1 (Pies 'n' Thighs on a Sunday) but frame it as: "This is what happens when a restaurant becomes part of the fabric of a neighborhood. Same families for 20 years. That's the kind of tenant you want."

#### Market Data

For sourced statistics on NYC restaurant under-penetration and full-service demand trends, see `core/context/cbh-investor-thesis.md`.

#### What to Avoid
- Over-emphasizing growth plans (sounds risky)
- Vague language about "concepts" or "ideas"
- Talking about restaurants you might acquire (stick to what you have)
- The personal backstory (they don't care—they care about rent checks)

### Audience 7: Tech/AI Curious People
*(Colleagues, friends, industry folks who ask "how do you use AI?")*

#### What They Actually Care About
- How AI works in practice, not theory
- What's actually possible for a small business
- Whether this is hype or real, proven results
- How technical you need to be to do this

#### Opening Hook

> "I sold a podcast company to Spotify. Now I run a fried chicken restaurant with a data warehouse that has half a million rows and a personal AI assistant that works while I sleep. Let me show you why."

#### Key Beats to Hit

- **Start with the Corner Booth story:** Use the curiosity gap. Podcast company to fried chicken is the hook that earns attention.
- **The problem:** "I run a small holding company with three partners. No analysts, no IT department, no data team. Every function that a large restaurant group has a whole department for, we handle ourselves."
- **The solution:** "I built what I call a personal operating system. It's an AI brain that runs 24/7 on a Mac Mini in my apartment. I can talk to it from my laptop or my phone. It handles my inbox, tracks my construction project, runs my data warehouse, and writes my weekly reports."
- **Show, don't tell:** Walk through the architecture. The personal OS diagram shows the brain system, the automated services, the MCP integrations. The data warehouse diagram shows 13 data sources flowing into a single database.
- **Real examples that land:**
  - "Every morning at 4 AM, while I'm sleeping, the system pulls yesterday's sales from our POS, weather data, labor costs, food cost invoices, and credit card transactions. By 5 AM it's written me a daily digest with my calendar and research on everyone I'm meeting. By 6 AM it's triaged my inbox."
  - "We're building out a second restaurant in Park Slope. The system monitors all my email, text, and meeting transcripts for construction-related updates and logs them to a shared project tracker. I never miss a message from the contractor."
  - "We run email marketing to 26,000 customers. The system scores every customer based on how often they order, what they spend, and whether they open our emails, then syncs those segments to Mailchimp automatically."
- **The punchline:** "I'm not a programmer. I can't write code. But these AI tools let me describe what I need in plain language and they build it. A year ago I couldn't do any of this."
- **The bigger point:** "The advantages of keeping a team and a company small will continue to grow." The tools are getting better every month. What required a department last year requires a prompt today. The ceiling on what a small, focused team can accomplish is rising fast, and it's not going to stop.

#### Concrete Scene to Use

The 4 AM automation run. Paint the picture of the Mac Mini humming away at 4 AM, pulling data from a dozen sources, writing reports, triaging email. You wake up and everything is organized. That's the scene that makes it tangible.

#### Status Matching

Slight underplay works best here. "I'm still figuring this out" and "I'm not technical" are both true and disarming. Don't oversell or make it sound like you've cracked the code. You're a restaurant operator who happens to be curious about tools. That's the honest frame.

#### Ultimate Concern

Their ultimate concern is relevance. They're wondering: should I be doing this? Am I falling behind? Your story should make them feel inspired, not intimidated. The message is: if a fried chicken restaurant owner can do this, anyone can.

#### What to Avoid
- Technical jargon (nobody cares about Supabase vs Postgres vs MySQL)
- Making it sound easy (acknowledge the learning curve, the failures, the things that broke)
- Overselling AI as a category (stay grounded in what you've actually built, not what's theoretically possible)
- Comparing to other companies or tools (this is your story, not a product review)

### The Three Cardinal Rules

1. **Concrete beats abstract.** "Pies 'n' Thighs" beats "beloved neighborhood restaurant." "20 years" beats "established." "Kids pressed against the glass" beats "strong community following."

2. **Match the message to the listener.** Investors need credibility. Restaurant owners need trust. Landlords need stability. Casual acquaintances need a picture.

3. **Let them ask for more.** Start short. If they lean in, give them more. The curiosity gap is your friend. Don't dump your whole story on someone who asked "what do you do?"

### Words to Kill

These words make your story forgettable. Replace them with specifics:

- "Holding company" → "I own a few restaurants"
- "Portfolio" → "Pies 'n' Thighs and Heaps"
- "Patient capital" → "We're in it for the long haul"
- "Beloved neighborhood brand" → "The fried chicken place that's been in Williamsburg for 20 years"
- "Untapped potential" → "They've never had the support to open a second location"
- "Acquisition-driven" → "I buy restaurants"
- "Community infrastructure" → "The place where the same families have been eating for 20 years"

### The One-Minute Version

If you need to tell the whole story in 60 seconds:

> *"I went to five different schools in five years as a kid. I never really belonged anywhere. When I sold Gimlet to Spotify, I had the resources to do something about that—not for me, but for neighborhoods that are losing the places that make them feel like home.*
>
> *Pies 'n' Thighs has been in Williamsburg for 20 years. Heaps is across the street from an elementary school—parents stop there every morning. These places are community infrastructure. But running one restaurant is almost impossible economically. I'm building a company that gives these places the support they need to last another 20 years—without turning them into chains.*
>
> *That's Corner Booth Holdings. We're the silent partner that lets neighborhood restaurants stay neighborhood restaurants."*

## Part 4: Advanced Techniques

### Reading the Room

Wolfgang talks about matching status and reading energy. You need to know if your story is landing so you can adjust in real-time.

#### Signs They're Engaged

- They lean forward physically
- They ask specific follow-up questions
- They connect your story to their experience ("Oh, like when I...")
- Their questions get more personal or detailed
- They want to know specifics: numbers, names, timelines

#### Signs You've Lost Them

- Checking phone
- Generic positive responses ("That's cool," "Interesting")
- Trying to change subject
- Eyes glazing over
- Their questions are about logistics, not meaning

#### What to Do

**If engaged:** Go deeper. Share more scenes. Let them pull it out of you. Follow their curiosity.

**If lost:** Cut it short. "Anyway, that's the high-level. What do you do?" Don't force it. Some people just aren't your audience, and that's fine.

### The Status Question

From Wolfgang: *"When he overplayed his status, he would be hated. When he underplayed his status, he wouldn't get what he wanted. When he matched status, he would get whatever he asked for."*

Before each conversation, ask yourself: **"What's the status dynamic here?"**

#### With Investors

**The dynamic:** You're equals. You have something they want (access to a contrarian bet), they have something you want (capital).

**How to match:** Neither supplicating nor arrogant. Confident but collaborative. You're offering them an opportunity, not begging for money.

**Warning signs:**
- Overplay: "I'm going to build the next Shake Shack" (arrogant)
- Underplay: "I know restaurants are risky but..." (apologetic)
- Match: "We're being selective about partners. Here's why this works."

#### With Restaurant Owners

**The dynamic:** You're the buyer, but they're the expert. They built something over decades. You're offering capital and support.

**How to match:** Slight deference to their craft and vision, but confidence in your capital and systems. You're not here to teach them their business—you're here to help them do it better.

**Warning signs:**
- Overplay: "Here's how we'll improve your operations" (condescending)
- Underplay: "I'm still learning this business" (too green)
- Match: "You've built something special. I want to help you keep it that way."

#### With Employees

**The dynamic:** You're the boss, but they're evaluating you too. Good people have options.

**How to match:** Show vision and opportunity without condescension. Respect their expertise. Make them feel part of something bigger.

**Warning signs:**
- Overplay: Top-down directives without context
- Underplay: Too much "we're all figuring this out together" (needs leadership)
- Match: "Here's where we're going. I need people who can help us get there."

#### With Landlords

**The dynamic:** They have power (property), you have credibility (track record, capital). It's a negotiation between equals.

**How to match:** Professional confidence. You're a good tenant, they're a good landlord. Mutual benefit.

**Warning signs:**
- Overplay: "We'll make your building more valuable" (presumptuous)
- Underplay: "We'd be grateful for the space" (weak negotiating position)
- Match: "We're looking for the right spaces for long-term tenants. Here's our track record."

#### With Industry Folks

**The dynamic:** Peer-to-peer. You're all in the trenches.

**How to match:** Humility about what you don't know, confidence about what you do. Ask questions. Share what's working.

**Warning signs:**
- Overplay: Acting like you've figured it all out
- Underplay: Playing dumb or apologizing for your Gimlet background
- Match: "I'm still learning the restaurant business, but here's what I'm seeing..."

**The Rule:** Never overplay your status (they'll resent you) or underplay it (you won't get what you want). Find the match point.

### The Ultimate Concern

Wolfgang says stories work when they speak to "ultimate concern"—the thing people are absolutely concerned about. For different audiences, Corner Booth speaks to different ultimate concerns:

#### Investors

**Their ultimate concern:** Being proven right about a contrarian bet others missed. The fear of missing the obvious-in-retrospect opportunity.

**How your story speaks to it:** "Everyone thinks restaurants are uninvestable. But patient capital in the right brands generates exceptional returns. You'll see this in five years and wish you'd gotten in now."

#### Restaurant Owners

**Their ultimate concern:** Losing what they built. Watching their life's work get destroyed by someone who doesn't understand it. Or conversely, never being able to retire because they can't sell without guilt.

**How your story speaks to it:** "You built something that matters. I'm not here to change it—I'm here to make sure it's still here in 20 years. And to give you options you don't have now."

#### Employees

**Their ultimate concern:** Working for something meaningful that also provides real growth and stability. Not selling out, but also not being stuck.

**How your story speaks to it:** "These aren't just jobs. This is about building places that matter to neighborhoods—and building real careers for people who care about hospitality."

#### Neighbors/Customers

**Their ultimate concern:** Losing the places that make their neighborhood feel like home. Watching beloved institutions close or get replaced by chains.

**How your story speaks to it:** "The places you love are hard to sustain. We're the reason they'll still be here when your kids grow up."

#### Landlords

**Their ultimate concern:** Stable, long-term tenants who pay rent and add value to the building/neighborhood. Not having to deal with turnover.

**How your story speaks to it:** "We're not a pop-up or an experiment. These brands have been around for 20+ years. We want to be in your building for another 20."

---

**Before you walk into any conversation, ask yourself: What is this person's ultimate concern? Then make sure your story speaks to it.**
