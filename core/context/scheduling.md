## Scheduling Preferences

Matt's calendar patterns and meeting preferences. Load this before proposing any meeting times in email drafts.

### The 2-Slot Rule

Always offer exactly 2 time slots: one preferred, one backup. Never 3+. Offering multiple open slots signals "I have a completely open day," which is not the impression Matt wants to give.

### Check the Calendar First

Before proposing any time, use `list-events` or `get-freebusy` to see what's actually on the calendar. Find slots adjacent to existing meetings (especially video calls). Never propose blind.

### Stack Video Calls

If Matt already has a video call on a given day, propose the new call adjacent to it (immediately before or after). Cluster video calls together on the same day rather than scattering them across the week.

### Protected Time

- **Monday before noon** is planning and deep work time. Never offer Monday morning as a first choice. Fine as a backup if nothing else works (soft protect).

### Weekly Pattern Reference

Use this to pick good default slots when the calendar is sparse:

- **Monday:** Lightest day. Meetings cluster 3-4 PM. Mornings open (protected). Good day to add a single call in the afternoon.
- **Tuesday:** Busy late morning (10-11 AM). Locked 1-3 PM internal block. Best external slots: 10 AM or 3:30 PM+.
- **Wednesday:** Afternoon-heavy (1-3 PM cluster). Mornings relatively open. Good for a morning external meeting.
- **Thursday:** Busiest day (most meetings per year). Noon locked (Sarah lunch). 3 PM often has 1:1s. Hardest day to find slots. Use only when stacking adjacent to existing calls.
- **Friday:** 11 AM locked (Heap's/TSCG). More remote/video calls. Lighter after the 11 AM block. Good for late morning (9-10 AM) or early afternoon calls.

### Default Durations

- **30 minutes** for calls, check-ins, quick syncs
- **60 minutes** for substantive meetings, working sessions, first meetings

### Travel Time Buffers (In-Person Meetings)

When proposing an in-person meeting (coffee, lunch, dinner, or any meeting with a physical location), check adjacent calendar events and leave enough travel buffer. Matt is based in Brooklyn.

| Scenario | Buffer |
|----------|--------|
| Within same neighborhood (e.g., both in Williamsburg) | 15 minutes |
| Between Brooklyn neighborhoods (e.g., Williamsburg to Park Slope) | 30 minutes |
| Brooklyn to Manhattan or vice versa | 45 minutes |
| Within Manhattan | 30 minutes |
| Adjacent meeting has no address/location listed | 30 minutes (safe default) |

**Rules:**
- Travel buffers apply before AND after the in-person meeting. Check both the prior and next event on the calendar.
- Video/phone calls don't need travel buffer (Matt can take them from anywhere).
- If proposing an in-person slot, the calendar must be clear for: travel buffer + meeting duration + travel buffer.
- When the meeting type is ambiguous, ask in the draft whether the person prefers in-person or a call, and propose slots that work for either.

### Networking and Casual Meetings

Let the other person's world guide the format. Don't default to Matt's usual spots. If they're a restaurant person, suggest visiting their place. If they're in a different city, propose meeting on their turf or near their office. Matt is curious about how people live and work.

### Deferred Commitments

When a "let's grab coffee when you're in [city]" type commitment comes up:

1. Create a Things task with the person's name and city as a deferred trigger
2. When a trip to that city appears on the calendar, draft outreach to that person with a specific proposed time and place
3. Present the draft for Matt's review before sending

### Geographic Clustering

Nice to have, not a rule. If Matt is already going to a specific neighborhood (e.g., Park Slope), consider suggesting other meetings in the same area on the same day. Don't force the calendar around geography.
